Target Keywords: ● full blood count explained ● blood test results interpretation ● CBC test meaning Full Blood Count Explained: Understanding Your Blood Test Results Have you ever had blood drawn and wondered what all those numbers on your lab report actually mean? A full blood count (FBC) – also called a complete blood count (CBC) – is one of the most common blood tests doctors order. Here's what it measures and why it matters. What Is a Full Blood Count? A full blood count is a simple blood test that measures three main types of cells in your blood: 1. Red blood cells (RBCs) – carry oxygen from your lungs to the rest of your body 2. White blood cells (WBCs) – fight infection and disease 3. Platelets – help your blood clot to stop bleeding The test also measures hemoglobin (the protein in red blood cells that carries oxygen) and hematocrit (the percentage of your blood made up of red blood cells). Red Blood Cells: Oxygen Delivery Your red blood cells contain hemoglobin, which binds to oxygen. When doctors look at your RBC results, they check: Measurement What It Means Low RBC count Possible anemia, iron deficiency, or blood loss High RBC count Possible dehydration, lung disease, or living at high altitude Normal ranges for adults: ● Men: 4.7–6.1 million cells per microliter ● Women: 4.2–5.4 million cells per microliter White Blood Cells: Your Immune System White blood cells are your body's defense team. There are five types of WBCs, each with a different job. A high WBC count often means your body is fighting an infection. A very low WBC count can put you at risk for infections. Normal WBC range: 4,500–11,000 cells per microliter Platelets: Clotting Control When you get a cut, platelets rush to the site and form a plug to stop bleeding. Low platelets can cause easy bruising or bleeding problems. High platelets may increase the risk of blood clots. Normal platelet range: 150,000–450,000 platelets per microliter When Would a Doctor Order This Test? Doctors commonly order a full blood count to: ● Check for anemia (fatigue, weakness, pale skin) ● Diagnose infections (fever, inflammation) ● Monitor cancer treatments (chemotherapy affects blood cells) ● Investigate unexplained bruising or bleeding ● Screen for blood disorders like leukemia What Abnormal Results Might Mean Abnormality Possible Causes Low RBC + low hemoglobin Iron deficiency anemia, vitamin B12 deficiency High WBC Bacterial infection, inflammation, stress response Low WBC Viral infection, bone marrow problem, autoimmune disease Low platelets Viral infection, medication side effect, autoimmune condition High platelets Inflammation, iron deficiency, reaction to infection Important Note A single abnormal result does not automatically mean you have a disease. Doctors look at your entire health picture – symptoms, medical history, and other tests – before making a diagnosis. Always discuss your results with a qualified healthcare provider. Target Keywords: ● photosynthesis explained ● how plants make food ● photosynthesis equation Photosynthesis Explained: How Plants Make Their Own Food Photosynthesis is the process plants use to turn sunlight into food. Without it, life on Earth would not exist – because photosynthesis produces the oxygen we breathe and the food we eat. The Simple Version Plants take in: ● Sunlight (energy) ● Water (through their roots) ● Carbon dioxide (from the air) They produce: ● Glucose (sugar for energy and growth) ● Oxygen (released into the air) The Chemical Equation 6CO₂ + 6H₂O + sunlight → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ In other words: Six molecules of carbon dioxide plus six molecules of water, using sunlight energy, produce one molecule of glucose and six molecules of oxygen. Where Photosynthesis Happens Photosynthesis occurs inside plant leaves in tiny structures called chloroplasts. Inside chloroplasts is a green pigment called chlorophyll – which is why plants are green. Chlorophyll absorbs sunlight (especially blue and red light) and reflects green light back to our eyes. Why Photosynthesis Matters for Humans 1. Oxygen production – Almost all the oxygen in Earth's atmosphere comes from photosynthesis. 2. Food source – Every animal on Earth depends either directly (eating plants) or indirectly (eating plant-eaters) on photosynthesis. 3. Carbon dioxide removal – Plants pull CO₂ from the atmosphere, helping to regulate Earth's climate. Fun Fact A single large tree can produce enough oxygen for a family of four people for one year.
